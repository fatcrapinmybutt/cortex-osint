## III. STANDARD OF REVIEW

### MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c) — Fraud by an Adverse Party

MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c) provides that a court may relieve a party from a final judgment on the basis of "fraud (intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party." Under Michigan law, fraud on the court occurs when an officer of the court — including an attorney — makes a deliberate misrepresentation to the court that affects the court's decision. *Triplett v St Amour*, 444 Mich 170 (1993). The insertion of res judicata language into an order the court never verbally ruled constitutes a deliberate misrepresentation by an officer of the court.

### MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) — Void Judgment

MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) provides for relief from a judgment that is void. A judgment is void if the court lacked subject matter jurisdiction or if it was procured through fraud. Because the dismissal order was premised on res judicata language fraudulently inserted by Mr. Brown — and because res judicata fails all four required elements as a matter of law — the judgment is void.

### Timing

MCR 2.612(C)(2) provides that a motion under (c) or (d) must be made within a "reasonable time." There is no absolute one-year bar for (c) and (d) grounds (unlike grounds (a), (b), which are subject to the one-year window). Plaintiff brings this motion within a reasonable time after discovering the full scope of Mr. Brown's fraudulent insertion.

## IV. ARGUMENT A — RES JUDICATA FAILS ALL FOUR REQUIRED ELEMENTS

Under *Adair v State of Michigan*, 470 Mich 105, 116–117, 680 NW2d 386 (2004), res judicata requires:

> (1) a final decision on the merits by a court of competent jurisdiction; (2) the same parties or their privies; (3) the same claims or causes of action; and (4) an opportunity to have been litigated in the first action.

All four elements fail here.

### Element 1: No Final Decision on the Merits

The prior proceeding involving Cricklewood MHP LLC was dismissed. A dismissal without prejudice is not a final decision on the merits and cannot support res judicata. *Jones v State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins Co*, 202 Mich App 393, 397 (1993) ("res judicata requires a judgment on the merits, not merely a dismissal"). No court has ever adjudicated the merits of Andrew Pigors' claims regarding VIN 1646732 or the post-eviction torts at issue in this action.

### Element 2: Different Parties — Cricklewood MHP LLC ≠ Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC

Res judicata requires identity of parties. *Adair*, 470 Mich at 121. Cricklewood MHP LLC is a New Jersey limited liability company. Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC is a **different** New Jersey limited liability company — separately registered, separately managed, with different principals. They are not the same entity. They are not in privity with one another for purposes of this claim (privity requires a legal relationship, not merely common ownership). The Cricklewood entity signed Andrew's lease; the Shady Oaks entity conducted the eviction. These are not the same parties.

### Element 3: Different Claims

The prior Cricklewood proceeding involved a lease dispute. The instant action involves: (a) wrongful eviction of a titled homeowner under MCL 125.2330; (b) conversion of manufactured home chattel; (c) post-eviction property destruction; (d) fraud and misrepresentation in the dismissal order; (e) LARA license violations under MCL 125.2301; and (f) RICO predicate acts. These claims did not exist, were not raised, and could not have been raised in the prior Cricklewood proceeding.

### Element 4: Dissolved LLC = Void Standing

Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC had no legal capacity to appear in court as a party. MCL 450.4802. A dissolved LLC cannot be a party in interest. Accordingly, there can be no identity of parties as a matter of law — one of the required parties did not legally exist.

## V. ARGUMENT B — JEREMY BROWN'S CONDUCT CONSTITUTES FRAUD ON THE COURT

An attorney who submits to the court an order containing substantive rulings that the court never made commits fraud on the court. *Triplett v St Amour*, 444 Mich 170 (1993); MRPC 3.3 (candor toward the tribunal); MRPC 3.4 (fairness to opposing party and counsel).

The specific fraud here is:

1. The Court did not orally rule on res judicata;
2. Mr. Brown prepared and submitted an order containing res judicata language;
3. The order, as entered, barred Plaintiff's claims on a basis the Court never adjudicated;
4. Plaintiff, as a pro se litigant, relied on the entered order as reflecting the Court's ruling;
5. Plaintiff's claims were thereby extinguished by attorney-manufactured language.

This conduct is independently sanctionable under MCR 2.114(D) (signing of pleadings — counsel certifies document is not filed for improper purpose). It also constitutes grounds for ARDC referral under MRPC 3.3.

The fact that Mr. Brown simultaneously caused a false forgery allegation to be leveled against Plaintiff — an allegation that was never prosecuted and has no evidentiary support — further demonstrates that Mr. Brown's strategy in this case was to fabricate barriers to Plaintiff's access to court, not to litigate the merits.

## VI. ARGUMENT C — THE FORGERY ALLEGATION WAS ABANDONED AND CANNOT STAND

In the course of this litigation, Defendants or their agents caused an accusatory notice to be placed on Plaintiff's door, alleging that Plaintiff forged documents or damaged property. Plaintiff possesses photographic evidence of this notice.

The critical undisputed facts are:

- **No criminal charges were ever filed** against Andrew James Pigors in connection with any forgery or property damage allegation arising from this matter.
- **No criminal conviction exists.**
- **No civil judgment of fraud has been entered against Plaintiff.**

Under Michigan law, a party cannot maintain a civil defense or claim based on an allegation of criminal wrongdoing that was never prosecuted. Moreover, leveling a forgery allegation in pleadings and then abandoning it constitutes an abuse of the judicial process. The abandoned allegation:

1. Cannot provide a basis for any affirmative defense;
2. Is itself evidence of Mr. Brown's bad faith in this litigation;
3. Supports Plaintiff's malicious prosecution and abuse of process claims; and
4. Demonstrates that Defendants' litigation strategy was designed to intimidate and prejudice, not to resolve a genuine legal dispute.
